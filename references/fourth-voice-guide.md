# Fourth Voice Guide

Rules for summarizing scraped content in Fourth's voice. Apply these whenever a skill produces a written summary, briefing, or KB entry from web-scraped material.

## What Fourth voice sounds like

**Confident and specific.** Fourth has 25+ years operating in hospitality workforce management. The voice reflects that. Claims are backed by specifics — named competitors, named customer segments, named data points — not vague positioning language.

**Hospitality-native.** The audience is operators: restaurant GMs, hotel HR directors, franchise owners. Write in their terms. "Tip pooling compliance" not "compensation management workflows." "Late-shift no-show rate" not "absence metric."

**Short and direct.** One claim per sentence. Active verbs. Front-load the finding. If a reader could skim the first sentence of every paragraph and understand the document, the structure is right.

**Data-backed where possible.** When a source cites a number, include it with attribution. When a source makes a claim without data, label it as such.

## What it avoids

- Vague superlatives: "best-in-class", "industry-leading", "world-class", "cutting-edge"
- Generic SaaS buzzwords: "synergy", "leverage", "unlock", "empower", "transform", "seamless"
- Defensive hedging: "we try to", "we strive to", "we aim to be"
- Passive attribution: "it has been noted that", "some experts believe"
- Inflated scope: "revolutionize", "disrupt", "redefine"
- Marketing-speak that sounds borrowed from a press release: "comprehensive solution", "end-to-end platform", "single source of truth" (unless quoting a competitor who uses this phrasing, in which case flag it)

## When summarizing competitor content

Describe what they say. Do not editorialize or add Fourth's counter-claims inside the summary itself.

- Attribute claims to the company: "R365 states that..." or "According to the Deputy pricing page..."
- Flag the difference between marketing claims and verifiable facts. Example: "7shifts claims 55,000 restaurants — this is stated on their homepage, not sourced from a third party."
- Keep Fourth's name out of the summary body unless you are explicitly writing a comparison or contrast section. Competitive summaries belong in the KB under `competitive/`; head-to-head comparisons belong in positioning documents.
- Note when pricing is gated or requires a sales call — do not invent numbers.

## When summarizing industry news

Lead with the fact. Then source. Then relevance.

Structure:
1. What happened (one sentence, plain language)
2. Source + date
3. Relevance to Fourth customer segments — restaurants, hotels, fine dining, QSR — or Fourth product areas (scheduling, payroll, tip management, compliance)

Example structure (not a template to copy verbatim):

> Aimbridge Hospitality signed a workforce management deal with UKG, according to a press release on ukg.com (2026-03-12). Aimbridge operates 1,400+ hotels, which makes this a signal that UKG is pushing further into mid-to-large hotel management companies — a segment where Fourth also competes.

Do not write "This is significant because..." as a sentence opener. Show the significance through specifics.

## Forbidden phrases

| Do not say | Say instead |
|------------|-------------|
| best-in-class | [specific metric or claim] |
| cutting-edge | [name the specific capability] |
| comprehensive solution | [list what it actually covers] |
| synergy | [describe the actual interaction] |
| empower operators | [describe what operators can now do] |
| seamless integration | [describe what integrates and how] |
| industry-leading | [cite the ranking or source] |
| transform your business | [state the specific outcome] |

## Examples

### Before (weak)

> R365 is a leading all-in-one restaurant management platform that empowers operators with best-in-class tools for accounting, inventory, and workforce. Their cutting-edge solution integrates seamlessly with existing POS systems.

### After (Fourth voice)

> Restaurant365 positions as an all-in-one platform covering accounting, inventory, scheduling, and payroll. Pricing is gated behind a demo form — no public per-location rates on the pricing page as of 2026-04-20. They claim 40,000+ restaurants on platform; this figure appears in marketing copy and has not been independently verified.

---

### Before (weak)

> There has been increased attention to tipping regulation in full-service restaurants, which could have implications for operators across the industry.

### After (Fourth voice)

> California's updated tip pool rules took effect January 2026, extending mandatory written tip pool agreements to chains with 25+ locations, according to HR Dive (2026-01-08). Relevant to Fourth customers in California with tipped hourly staff — particularly FSR and casual-dining chains where tip pooling is common.

---

### Before (weak)

> Hotel Management reported some interesting news about workforce technology adoption in the hospitality sector.

### After (Fourth voice)

> Hotel Management reported that 68% of hotel operators surveyed plan to expand AI scheduling tools in 2026, up from 41% in 2024 (Hotel Management, 2026-02-14). The cited source is a Hospitality Technology survey — methodology not detailed in the article.
